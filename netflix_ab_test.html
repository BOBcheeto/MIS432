Does Artwork Change What You Watch?

01 · The Business Problem

Why Netflix Obsesses Over Thumbnails

Every time you open Netflix, the platform is silently running hundreds of experiments. One of the most consequential: which thumbnail image is shown for each title. The same show might display a close-up of a dramatic face for one viewer and an action scene for another — all in real time, all driven by data.

This matters because Netflix's internal research has found that a viewer decides whether to click a title in roughly 1.8 seconds. The artwork is not decoration. It is the product. A thumbnail that earns a 30% click-through rate instead of 24% — a difference of just six people per hundred — translates into millions of additional streams when applied at Netflix's global scale. That is the difference between a show being perceived as a hit and being quietly cancelled.

The core business question this project addresses: How does a company like Netflix determine, with confidence, that one piece of artwork is genuinely better than another — and not just luckier? The answer is A/B testing, and the answer to "is it real?" is statistics.

02 · The Method

Experimentation as a Business Discipline

An A/B test is one of the most powerful tools in modern business. The idea is deceptively simple: take a large group of real users, randomly split them into two groups, show each group a different version of something, and measure what happens.

Random assignment is the key ingredient. Because users are randomly sorted — not by age, location, or viewing habits — the two groups are statistically identical at the start. Any difference in behavior at the end can only be explained by the one thing that was different: the artwork. This is what allows a business to say "Artwork B caused more clicks," not just "Artwork B happened to get more clicks."

Netflix runs this kind of experiment across virtually every element of its product — artwork, trailers, recommendation order, email subject lines, even the shade of red on the play button. This culture of continuous, data-driven experimentation is a core reason Netflix outperforms competitors that rely on gut instinct and creative opinion. It is also a direct application of machine learning thinking: form a hypothesis, collect data, evaluate evidence, update decisions.

01

Formulate a Hypothesis

Netflix's creative team believes Artwork B — perhaps a more emotionally expressive still — will outperform the current default, Artwork A. They define success as a higher click-through rate.

02

Randomly Assign Users

The engineering platform hashes user IDs to buckets. Half the eligible audience sees Artwork A; the other half sees Artwork B. No user sees both. No user self-selects their group.

03

Collect Behavioral Data

Every impression — every time the artwork appeared on screen — and every resulting click is logged. The raw data becomes a table: user ID, which artwork, did they click.

04

Test for Statistical Significance

A statistical test determines whether the observed difference in click rates is large enough — relative to the sample size — to conclude it reflects a real pattern rather than random chance.

05

Make a Data-Driven Decision

If the test clears the significance threshold, the winning variant is shipped to 100% of users. If not, the experiment runs longer or a new hypothesis is formed.

03 · The Code

Building the Experiment in Python

The following Python code replicates this entire workflow from scratch — simulation, analysis, visualization, and statistical testing — in a single script. Each of the four parts mirrors a step in Netflix's real experimentation pipeline.

Python · Google Colab 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats
from statsmodels.stats.proportion import proportions_ztest

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# PART 1 — Simulate the Experiment
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
np.random.seed(42)

n_users       = 1000
click_prob_A  = 0.24
click_prob_B  = 0.30

user_ids    = np.arange(1, n_users + 1)
assignments = np.random.choice(['A', 'B'], size=n_users, p=[0.5, 0.5])

clicks = np.where(
    assignments == 'A',
    np.random.binomial(1, click_prob_A, size=n_users),
    np.random.binomial(1, click_prob_B, size=n_users)
)

df = pd.DataFrame({
    'user_id':         user_ids,
    'artwork_version': assignments,
    'clicked':         clicks
})

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# PART 2 — Summary Metrics
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
summary = df.groupby('artwork_version')['clicked'].agg(
    total_users  = 'count',
    total_clicks = 'sum'
).reset_index()
summary['click_through_rate'] = summary['total_clicks'] / summary['total_users']

ctr_A    = summary.loc[summary['artwork_version'] == 'A', 'click_through_rate'].values[0]
ctr_B    = summary.loc[summary['artwork_version'] == 'B', 'click_through_rate'].values[0]
users_A  = summary.loc[summary['artwork_version'] == 'A', 'total_users'].values[0]
users_B  = summary.loc[summary['artwork_version'] == 'B', 'total_users'].values[0]
clicks_A = summary.loc[summary['artwork_version'] == 'A', 'total_clicks'].values[0]
clicks_B = summary.loc[summary['artwork_version'] == 'B', 'total_clicks'].values[0]
lift     = (ctr_B - ctr_A) / ctr_A * 100

print("=" * 50)
print("          A/B Test Summary Results")
print("=" * 50)
print(f"{'Artwork':<12} {'Users':>8} {'Clicks':>8} {'CTR':>10}")
print("-" * 50)
for _, row in summary.iterrows():
    print(f"{row['artwork_version']:<12} {int(row['total_users']):>8} "
          f"{int(row['total_clicks']):>8} {row['click_through_rate']:>9.1%}")
print("=" * 50)
print(f"\n  Relative lift of B over A: {lift:+.1f}%")
print("=" * 50)

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# PART 3 — Bar Chart
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#0F0F0F')
ax.set_facecolor('#0F0F0F')

artworks  = summary['artwork_version'].tolist()
ctrs      = summary['click_through_rate'].tolist()
colors    = ['#E50914', '#831010']
bar_width = 0.45

bars = ax.bar(artworks, ctrs, color=colors, width=bar_width,
              edgecolor='white', linewidth=0.6, zorder=3)

for bar, ctr in zip(bars, ctrs):
    ax.text(
        bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
        bar.get_height() + 0.004,
        f'{ctr:.1%}',
        ha='center', va='bottom',
        color='white', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold'
    )

ax.annotate(
    f'Relative lift: {lift:+.1f}%',
    xy=(1, ctr_B), xytext=(1.32, ctr_B),
    fontsize=10, color='#FFD700', fontweight='bold',
    arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle='->', color='#FFD700', lw=1.5),
    va='center'
)

ax.yaxis.grid(True, color='#333333', linewidth=0.7, zorder=0)
ax.set_axisbelow(True)
ax.set_ylim(0, max(ctrs) * 1.35)
ax.set_ylabel('Click-Through Rate (CTR)', color='white', fontsize=11, labelpad=10)
ax.set_xlabel('Artwork Version',          color='white', fontsize=11, labelpad=10)
ax.set_title('Netflix Artwork A/B Test\nClick-Through Rate by Variant',
             color='white', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold', pad=15)

ax.tick_params(colors='white', labelsize=11)
for spine in ax.spines.values():
    spine.set_edgecolor('#444444')

ax.yaxis.set_major_formatter(plt.FuncFormatter(lambda y, _: f'{y:.0%}'))

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('ab_test_ctr.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='#0F0F0F')
plt.show()

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# PART 4 — Statistical Significance Test
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
count  = np.array([clicks_B, clicks_A])
nobs   = np.array([users_B,  users_A])

z_stat, p_value = proportions_ztest(count, nobs)

confidence_level = 0.95
alpha            = 1 - confidence_level
significant      = p_value < alpha

ci_A = stats.binom.interval(confidence_level, users_A, ctr_A)
ci_B = stats.binom.interval(confidence_level, users_B, ctr_B)
ci_A_pct = (ci_A[0] / users_A, ci_A[1] / users_A)
ci_B_pct = (ci_B[0] / users_B, ci_B[1] / users_B)

print("\n" + "=" * 50)
print("       Two-Proportion Z-Test Results")
print("=" * 50)
print(f"  Z-statistic       : {z_stat:>10.4f}")
print(f"  P-value           : {p_value:>10.4f}")
print(f"  Significance level: {alpha:>10.2f}  (95% confidence)")
print("-" * 50)
print(f"  CTR A: {ctr_A:.1%}  |  95% CI: [{ci_A_pct[0]:.1%} – {ci_A_pct[1]:.1%}]")
print(f"  CTR B: {ctr_B:.1%}  |  95% CI: [{ci_B_pct[0]:.1%} – {ci_B_pct[1]:.1%}]")
print("-" * 50)

if significant:
    print(f"\n  ✅ SIGNIFICANT — p={p_value:.4f} < α={alpha:.2f}")
    print("  The difference is unlikely to be due to chance.")
    print("  Recommendation: Ship Artwork B.")
else:
    print(f"\n  ❌ NOT SIGNIFICANT — p={p_value:.4f} ≥ α={alpha:.2f}")
    print("  The difference could be due to chance.")
    print("  Recommendation: Do not ship. Gather more data.")

What the 6-Point Gap Means

Six percentage points may sound small, but it represents a 25% improvement relative to Artwork A's baseline. In a platform with 300 million subscribers, a 25% lift in click-through rate on a major title translates into tens of millions of additional streams — and streams are directly correlated with subscriber retention, the metric Netflix cares about most.

The Statistical Test Confirmed It

The two-proportion z-test returned a p-value below 0.05 — the standard threshold for declaring a result statistically significant at 95% confidence. In plain English: there is less than a 5% probability that the gap between Artwork A and Artwork B appeared by random chance. The experiment had enough users, and the difference was large enough, to conclude with confidence that Artwork B is the genuinely better performer.

05 · Broader Context

How This Connects to AI and Machine Learning

This experiment is not just a one-off test — it is the foundation layer of Netflix's machine learning infrastructure. Every A/B test produces labeled outcome data: a user saw X, and either clicked or did not. At scale, this data trains the recommendation models that decide which titles appear on your homepage, in what order, and with which artwork.

Experimentation is how machine learning systems learn what works in the real world. A model might predict that a certain thumbnail will perform well based on historical patterns. An A/B test verifies whether that prediction holds with real users. The confirmed results then feed back into the model, improving future predictions. This cycle — hypothesis, test, measure, retrain — is the operational heartbeat of AI-driven product development.

Netflix's system, called Exploratory Data Analysis and Experimentation (XP), runs thousands of these experiments simultaneously. Each one is a structured learning event. The business impact compounds: better artwork leads to more clicks, more clicks generate more viewing data, more viewing data trains better recommendation models, better recommendations increase retention, and higher retention sustains the subscription revenue that funds more content. A/B testing is not a statistics exercise — it is a revenue engine.

06 · Business Recommendation

What Netflix Should Do

Ship Artwork B to 100% of Users.

The data is clear and the conclusion is statistically defensible. Artwork B generated a click-through rate of approximately 30% compared to 24% for Artwork A — a relative improvement of 25%. The two-proportion z-test confirmed this difference is real, not a product of random variation, at the 95% confidence level.

The path forward for Netflix is to retire Artwork A as the default for this title and replace it with Artwork B globally. Given Netflix's scale, this single change is projected to meaningfully increase total plays for the title — reinforcing its perceived success within the platform's recommendation algorithm and increasing the likelihood that it surfaces to new users organically.

Beyond this title, the methodology itself should be noted: the margin of improvement found here (25% relative lift) is unusually large for a mature product. That suggests the current default artwork library may contain significant untested inventory. A systematic audit of high-traffic titles using the same experimental framework could unlock material engagement gains across the catalog at low marginal cost.

Confidence level: 95%  ·  p < 0.05  ·  n = 1,000  ·  Two-proportion z-test
